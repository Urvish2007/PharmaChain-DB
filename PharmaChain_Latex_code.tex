\section{Executive Summary}
\textbf{PostgreSQL} and \textbf{PL/pgSQL}, this project demonstrates strong \textbf{backend database design}, \textbf{secure data handling}, and \textbf{automation} for pharmaceutical manufacturing systems.

The main goal of \textbf{PharmaChain-DB} is to move important \textbf{business logic} directly into the \textbf{database} instead of depending heavily on \textbf{application code}. The system uses smart \textbf{database triggers}, well-structured \textbf{normalized tables}, and \textbf{stored procedures} to automatically enforce \textbf{FDA compliance rules}, manage \textbf{real-time inventory}, and handle \textbf{emergency product recalls} safely and efficiently.


\subsection{Comprehensive Entity Dictionary \& Use Cases}
To accurately model a highly regulated pharmaceutical factory, the database is divided into specialized tables across five distinct operational zones. Here is the complete breakdown of every entity, its role, and its business value:

\subsubsection*{1. Master Registries (The Ground Truth)}
These tables act as the foundational dictionaries of the system. Nothing can happen in the factory unless it is first registered here.

\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Material\_Master}:} The catalog of raw chemical ingredients (APIs and Excipients).
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Tracks critical safety data like hazardous status and storage conditions before chemicals ever enter the building.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Account\_Master}:} The directory of all external business partners (Suppliers, Distributors, Hospitals).
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Links financial invoices to real-world companies so the business knows exactly who they are buying from and selling to.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Employee\_Master}:} The directory of factory staff and their roles.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Provides accountability. Every lab test or machine operation is permanently tagged to a specific employee's ID.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Equipment\_Master}:} The registry of all physical machines on the factory floor.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Tracks calibration dates and maintenance statuses to prevent production from running on broken equipment.

    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Product\_Master}:} The official catalog of finished medicines the company produces.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Ensures the factory only manufactures approved, legally registered drugs.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{2. Financials \& Logistics}
These tables handle the money, legal agreements, and corporate accounting.

\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Transactions}:} The unified financial ledger for both buying and selling.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Centralizes all corporate accounting into one place to easily track cash flow, invoice statuses, and revenue without data duplication.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Supplier\_Contract}:} The legal agreements locked in with raw material vendors.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Prevents the purchasing team from buying chemicals at the wrong price or using an expired legal contract.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{3. Inventory Pipeline}
These tables manage the physical storage and safety screening of incoming raw materials.

\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Warehouse}:} The real-time physical storage tracker utilizing serialized unique key pairing.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Gives managers an exact count of what is on the shelves, enabling automated alerts when stock drops below safe reorder levels.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{RM\_Transaction} (Raw Material):} The link between a purchased raw material and its financial invoice.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Allows auditors to calculate exactly how much money is tied up in raw chemicals sitting in the warehouse.

    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Material\_Quality\_Check}:} Laboratory test results for newly purchased raw chemicals.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Stops contaminated, expired, or low-purity chemicals from ever reaching the factory mixing machines.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{4. Production Floor}
These tables track the actual creation of the medicine on the factory floor.

\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Formula\_Master}:} The exact chemical recipe (Bill of Materials) for a medicine.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Dictates the precise ratio of raw materials required, preventing operators from mixing the wrong chemicals together.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Batch}:} The central hub of the database; it tracks a specific production run of a medicine.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Provides absolute traceability. It calculates the expiry date, tracks how many pills were yielded, and acts as the bridge between raw materials and final sales.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Material\_Dispensing}:} Records the exact moment a worker takes raw materials out of the warehouse to put into a batch.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} This table triggers the automated stock deduction, instantly updating warehouse inventory without requiring human math.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Production\_Log}:} A timestamped diary of machine usage featuring asynchronous clock validation.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Allows managers to track processing bottlenecks by measuring exactly how long each manufacturing stage takes.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{5. Post-Production Compliance}
These tables enforce FDA regulations, track equipment health, and handle the medicine leaving the factory.

\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Product\_Quality\_Check}:} The final laboratory test results for newly manufactured medicine batches.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} The ultimate gatekeeper. This table holds the PASSED/FAILED grades that determine if a medicine is legally safe to leave the factory.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{FG\_Transaction} (Finished Goods):} Records the sale of finished medicine to hospitals or distributors.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Automatically triggers the Sales Compliance Blocker trigger. If the linked batch failed its lab test, this table rejects the sale entirely.
    
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Product\_Recall}:} The emergency quarantine registry.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} In the event of a safety crisis, this allows executives to instantly log the recall, quarantine the lab records, and drop physical warehouse stock to zero to prevent further harm.

    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{\underline{Maintenance\_Log}:} The historical record of machine repairs.
    \\ \textbf{Use Case:} Helps the finance team calculate which machines are costing the most money to keep running.
\end{itemize}

\section{Normalization and Functional Dependencies}
To guarantee data integrity and completely eliminate insert, update, and delete anomalies, the entire database architecture has been mathematically verified to satisfy Boyce-Codd Normal Form (BCNF). Below are the functional dependency (FD) proofs for every entity in the system.

% --- ZONE 1: MASTER REGISTRIES ---
\subsection{Master Registries}

\subsubsection{Table: Material\_Master}
Let $R = \{ \text{Material\_ID, Material\_Name, UOM, Storage\_Condition, isHazardous} \}$
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(a)] \textbf{Inferred FDs:} $\text{Material\_ID} \rightarrow \text{Material\_Name, UOM, Storage\_Condition, isHazardous}$
    \item[(b)] \textbf{Minimal Set of FDs:} 
    \\ $\text{Material\_ID} \rightarrow \text{Material\_Name}$
    \\ $\text{Material\_ID} \rightarrow \text{UOM}$
    \\ $\text{Material\_ID} \rightarrow \text{Storage\_Condition}$
    \\ $\text{Material\_ID} \rightarrow \text{isHazardous}$
    \item[(c)] \textbf{Keys:} Primary Key = $\{ \text{Material\_ID} \}$
    \item[(d)] \textbf{Proof of BCNF:} For every non-trivial FD ($X \rightarrow Y$), the determinant $X$ ($\text{Material\_ID}$) is a candidate key. No partial or transitive dependencies exist.
    \item[(e)] \textbf{Statement:} The table is in BCNF because all attributes depend strictly on the complete primary key.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Table: Account\_Master}
Let $R = \{ \text{Account\_No, Account\_Name, Account\_Type, Phone\_No, Email} \}$
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(a)] \textbf{Inferred FDs:} $\text{Account\_No} \rightarrow \text{Account\_Name, Account\_Type, Phone\_No, Email}$
    \item[(b)] \textbf{Minimal Set of FDs:} 
    \\ $\text{Account\_No} \rightarrow \text{Account\_Name}$
    \\ $\text{Account\_No} \rightarrow \text{Account\_Type}$
    \\ $\text{Account\_No} \rightarrow \text{Phone\_No}$
    \\ $\text{Account\_No} \rightarrow \text{Email}$
    \item[(c)] \textbf{Keys:} Primary Key = $\{ \text{Account\_No} \}$
    \item[(d)] \textbf{Proof of BCNF:} The only determinant in the relation is Account\_No, which uniquely identifies the entire tuple.
    \item[(e)] \textbf{Statement:} The table is in BCNF as there are no dependencies on non-prime attributes.
\end{enumerate}

Let $R = \{ \text{Recall\_ID, Batch\_No, Date\_Initiated, Reason, Qty\_Recalled} \}$
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(a)] \textbf{Inferred FDs:} $\text{Recall\_ID} \rightarrow \text{Batch\_No, Date\_Initiated, Reason, Qty\_Recalled}$
    \item[(b)] \textbf{Minimal Set of FDs:} 
    \\ $\text{Recall\_ID} \rightarrow \text{Batch\_No}$
    \\ $\text{Recall\_ID} \rightarrow \text{Date\_Initiated}$
    \\ $\text{Recall\_ID} \rightarrow \text{Reason}$
    \\ $\text{Recall\_ID} \rightarrow \text{Qty\_Recalled}$
    \item[(c)] \textbf{Keys:} Primary Key = $\{ \text{Recall\_ID} \}$
    \item[(d)] \textbf{Proof of BCNF:} Recall\_ID is the singular superkey determining the parameters of the emergency event.
    \item[(e)] \textbf{Statement:} The table is perfectly normalized to BCNF.
\end{enumerate}

The following SQL script defines the physical schema, data types, primary keys, foreign key constraints (with cascading rules), and domain constraints (\texttt{CHECK} clauses) for the entire PostgreSQL database.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=SQL, caption=Complete DDL Script for PharmaChain-DB Architecture]
CREATE SCHEMA IF NOT EXISTS pharma_manufacturing;

SET search_path TO pharma_manufacturing;

-- 1. Master Tables
CREATE TABLE Material_Master (
    Material_ID          VARCHAR(20)   PRIMARY KEY,
    Material_Name        VARCHAR(30)   NOT NULL,
    Material_Type        VARCHAR(20)   NOT NULL,
    Storage_Condition    VARCHAR(100)  NOT NULL,
    Shelf_Life           NUMERIC(3)    NOT NULL CHECK (Shelf_Life > 0),
    Therapeutic_Category VARCHAR(30)   NOT NULL,
    Material_State       VARCHAR(10)   NOT NULL,
    isHazardous          BOOLEAN       NOT NULL,
    isInflammable        BOOLEAN       NOT NULL,
    UOM                  VARCHAR(3)    NOT NULL,
    Reorder_Level        NUMERIC(10)   DEFAULT 1000 CHECK (Reorder_Level > 0)

CREATE TABLE Account_Master (

    Account_Name VARCHAR(50)  NOT NULL,
    Phone_No     VARCHAR(13)  NOT NULL,

CREATE TABLE Employee_Master (
    Emp_ID       VARCHAR(20) PRIMARY KEY,
    Emp_Name     VARCHAR(50) NOT NULL,
    Department   VARCHAR(30) NOT NULL,

CREATE TABLE Equipment_Master (

    Equipment_Name        VARCHAR(50) NOT NULL,

CREATE TABLE Product_Master (

    Product_Name     VARCHAR(20) NOT NULL,
    Generic_Name     VARCHAR(100) NOT NULL,
    Product_Type     VARCHAR(20) NOT NULL,
    Packing_Type     VARCHAR(10) NOT NULL,
    Packing_Size     VARCHAR(5)  NOT NULL,
    SalableorSample  VARCHAR(1)  NOT NULL CHECK (SalableorSample IN ('M','S')),

CREATE TABLE Transactions (
    Invoice_No       NUMERIC(10)   PRIMARY KEY,
    Transaction_Date DATE          NOT NULL,
    Currency         VARCHAR(3)    NOT NULL,
    Transaction_Type VARCHAR(4)    NOT NULL CHECK (Transaction_Type IN ('buy','sell')),
    Paid_Received    BOOLEAN       NOT NULL,
    Account_No       VARCHAR(11)   REFERENCES Account_Master(Account_No) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,

CREATE TABLE Supplier_Contract (
    Contract_ID   VARCHAR(20) PRIMARY KEY,

    Agreed_Price  NUMERIC(10,2) NOT NULL CHECK (Agreed_Price > 0),
    Valid_Until   DATE NOT NULL
);

-- 3. Warehouse & Inventory
CREATE TABLE Warehouse (
    Item_ID     BIGINT GENERATED ALWAYS AS IDENTITY PRIMARY KEY,
    Material_ID VARCHAR(20) NOT NULL REFERENCES Material_Master(Material_ID) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,
    Invoice_No  NUMERIC(10) NOT NULL REFERENCES Transactions(Invoice_No) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,
    UT_Q_A      VARCHAR(2)  NOT NULL,
    Stock       NUMERIC(10) NOT NULL CHECK (Stock > 0),
    CONSTRAINT uq_warehouse_mat_inv UNIQUE (Material_ID, Invoice_No)
);

CREATE TABLE RM_Transaction (
    Invoice_No NUMERIC(10)   NOT NULL REFERENCES Transactions(Invoice_No) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,
    Item_ID    BIGINT        NOT NULL REFERENCES Warehouse(Item_ID) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,
    RM_Qty     NUMERIC(10)   NOT NULL CHECK (RM_Qty > 0),
    Val        NUMERIC(10,2) NOT NULL CHECK (Val > 0),
    CONSTRAINT pk_rm_transaction PRIMARY KEY (Invoice_No, Item_ID)
);

CREATE TABLE Material_Quality_Check (
    Report_ID     VARCHAR(20)  PRIMARY KEY,
    Item_ID       BIGINT       NOT NULL,
    Analysis_Date DATE         NOT NULL,
    Analyst_Name  VARCHAR(20)  NOT NULL,
    Sample_Size   NUMERIC(10)  NOT NULL CHECK (Sample_Size > 0),
    Test          VARCHAR(20)  NOT NULL,
    Limits        VARCHAR(20)  NOT NULL,
    Results       VARCHAR(30)  NOT NULL,
    Emp_ID        VARCHAR(20)  REFERENCES Employee_Master(Emp_ID) ON DELETE SET NULL,
    CONSTRAINT fk_mqc_warehouse FOREIGN KEY (Item_ID) REFERENCES Warehouse(Item_ID) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE
);

-- 4. Manufacturing & Production
CREATE TABLE Formula_Master (
    Product_ID        VARCHAR(20) NOT NULL REFERENCES Product_Master(Product_ID) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,
    Material_ID       VARCHAR(20) NOT NULL REFERENCES Material_Master(Material_ID) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,
    Weight_per_tablet NUMERIC(10) NOT NULL CHECK (Weight_per_tablet > 0),
    CONSTRAINT pk_formula_master PRIMARY KEY (Product_ID, Material_ID)
);

CREATE TABLE Batch (
    Batch_No         NUMERIC(10) PRIMARY KEY,
    Batch_Size       NUMERIC(10) NOT NULL CHECK (Batch_Size > 0),
    Mfg_Date         DATE        NOT NULL,
    Exp_Date         DATE,
    Product_ID       VARCHAR(20) REFERENCES Product_Master(Product_ID) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,
    Stock_Qty        NUMERIC(10) NOT NULL CHECK (Stock_Qty >= 0),
    UT_Q_A           VARCHAR(2)  NOT NULL,
    Yield_Percentage NUMERIC(5,2) DEFAULT 98.50 CHECK (Yield_Percentage >= 0 AND Yield_Percentage <= 100)

CREATE TABLE Material_Dispensing (
    Batch_No         NUMERIC(10) NOT NULL REFERENCES Batch(Batch_No) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,
    Item_ID          BIGINT      NOT NULL REFERENCES Warehouse(Item_ID) ON DELETE CASCADE ON UPDATE CASCADE,

CREATE TABLE Production_Log (

    Analyst_Name  VARCHAR(20)  NOT NULL,
    Sample_Size   NUMERIC(10)  NOT NULL CHECK (Sample_Size > 0),
    Process_State VARCHAR(20)  NOT NULL,
    Test          VARCHAR(20)  NOT NULL,
    Limits        VARCHAR(20)  NOT NULL,

\subsection*{1. Revenue Analytics: "Which products are currently making us the most money?"}

\textbf{Relational Algebra:}
\vspace{0.2cm}\\
\noindent\fbox{%
\begin{minipage}{0.95\textwidth}
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
    & \gamma_{\text{Product\_Name, Product\_Type, COUNT(Batch\_No), SUM(Sale\_Qty), SUM(Val)}} \\
    & \quad (\text{FG\_Transaction} \bowtie \text{Batch} \bowtie \text{Product\_Master})
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
\end{minipage}}
\vspace{0.2cm}

\begin{lstlisting}
SELECT 
    pm.Product_Name,
    pm.Product_Type,
    COUNT(DISTINCT fgt.Batch_No) AS Batches_Sold,
    SUM(fgt.Sale_Qty) AS Total_Units_Sold,
    SUM(fgt.Val) AS Total_Revenue_INR
FROM FG_Transaction fgt
JOIN Batch b ON fgt.Batch_No = b.Batch_No
JOIN Product_Master pm ON b.Product_ID = pm.Product_ID
GROUP BY pm.Product_Name, pm.Product_Type
ORDER BY Total_Revenue_INR DESC;
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Business Significance:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] Identifies high-margin pharmaceutical products driving the company's primary revenue.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] Guides future production planning by highlighting the most demanded medicines.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] Evaluates financial performance across different physical product types (e.g., Tablets vs. Syrups).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{1. The Warehouse Automator (\texttt{trg\_deduct\_stock\_on\_dispense})}
\textbf{Execution Phase:} \texttt{AFTER INSERT} on \texttt{Material\_Dispensing}

\textbf{Why is this important?}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Zero Manual Math:} The moment a factory worker logs that they have taken raw materials for a medicine batch, this trigger automatically does the subtraction in the background. It instantly updates the physical warehouse stock without needing a human to remember to do it.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{The Negative Stock Fail-Safe:} It acts as a hard security guard. If someone accidentally types an extra zero and tries to take out more stock than what actually exists on the shelf, the trigger catches it and \textbf{blocks the transaction entirely}. This guarantees the database never shows impossible "negative" inventory.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
-- 
-- FUNCTION: Automate Warehouse Stock Deduction
-- DESCRIPTION: When a batch takes material, deduct it from the warehouse.
--              If there isn't enough stock, block the transaction.
-- 
CREATE OR REPLACE FUNCTION automate_warehouse_stock()
RETURNS TRIGGER 
LANGUAGE plpgsql
AS $$
DECLARE
    v_current_stock NUMERIC(10);
BEGIN
    -- 1. Find out how much stock we currently have for this specific item
    SELECT Stock INTO v_current_stock 
    FROM Warehouse 
    WHERE Item_ID = NEW.Item_ID;

    -- 2. Safety Check: Do we have enough stock to fulfill this request?
    IF v_current_stock < NEW.Quantity_Issued THEN
        RAISE EXCEPTION 'Transaction Blocked: Not enough stock! Item % only has % units left, but you tried to dispense %.', 
                        NEW.Item_ID, v_current_stock, NEW.Quantity_Issued;
    END IF;

    -- 3. If we have enough stock, safely deduct it from the Warehouse
    UPDATE Warehouse
    SET Stock = Stock - NEW.Quantity_Issued
    WHERE Item_ID = NEW.Item_ID;

    -- 4. Allow the original INSERT into Material_Dispensing to finish
    RETURN NEW;
END;
$$;

CREATE TRIGGER trg_deduct_stock_on_dispense
AFTER INSERT ON Material_Dispensing
FOR EACH ROW
EXECUTE FUNCTION automate_warehouse_stock();
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{2. The FDA Compliance Blocker (\texttt{trg\_prevent\_bad\_sales})}
\textbf{Execution Phase:} \texttt{BEFORE INSERT} on \texttt{FG\_Transaction}

\textbf{Why is this important?}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{The Ultimate Safety Net:} This trigger acts as the final gatekeeper before medicine leaves the factory. Whenever a hospital or distributor attempts to purchase a batch, the database automatically cross-references the official Quality Control lab results in real-time.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Absolute Enforcement:} If a batch failed its lab test, or if it simply hasn't been tested yet, the trigger throws a hard exception. It makes it technologically impossible to sell toxic or unverified medicine, ensuring strict FDA compliance regardless of human error or frontend software bugs.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
-- 
-- FUNCTION: FDA Compliance Enforcer
-- DESCRIPTION: Prevents the sale of any batch in FG_Transaction if it 
--              failed Quality Control or hasn't been tested yet.
-- 

CREATE OR REPLACE FUNCTION enforce_quality_control()
RETURNS TRIGGER 
LANGUAGE plpgsql
AS $$
DECLARE
    v_qa_result VARCHAR(30);
BEGIN
    -- 1. Look up the official lab result for the batch they are trying to sell
    SELECT Results INTO v_qa_result
    FROM Product_Quality_Check
    WHERE Batch_No = NEW.Batch_No;

    -- 2. Safety Rule A: Has it been tested at all?
    IF v_qa_result IS NULL THEN
        RAISE EXCEPTION 'COMPLIANCE BLOCK: Batch % has not been tested by the QC Lab yet. Sale is illegal!', NEW.Batch_No;
    END IF;

    -- 3. Safety Rule B: Did it fail the test?
    IF v_qa_result = 'FAILED' THEN
        RAISE EXCEPTION 'SAFETY BLOCK: Batch % FAILED quality control. This batch must be quarantined, not sold!', NEW.Batch_No;
    END IF;

    -- 4. If it exists and didn't fail (i.e., 'PASSED'), allow the sale to proceed
    RETURN NEW;
END;
$$;

-- Bind the trigger to fire BEFORE the sale is finalized
CREATE TRIGGER trg_prevent_bad_sales
BEFORE INSERT ON FG_Transaction
FOR EACH ROW
EXECUTE FUNCTION enforce_quality_control();
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{3. The Timeline Enforcer (\texttt{trg\_strict\_batch\_dates})}
\textbf{Execution Phase:} \texttt{BEFORE INSERT OR UPDATE} on \texttt{Batch}

\textbf{Why is this important?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Preventing Time Travel:} It acts as a strict chronological safeguard. If a data-entry worker accidentally types the wrong year, or attempts to fraudulently forward-date a batch to extend its shelf life, the database \textbf{blocks the entry immediately}.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Shelf-Life Compliance:} Distributors and hospitals will reject medicine that expires too quickly. This trigger mathematically guarantees that no medicine can even be registered in the system unless it possesses a \textbf{strict minimum of a 6-month viability window}, protecting the brand's supply chain standards.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
-- 
-- FUNCTION: Strict Batch Date Enforcer
-- DESCRIPTION: Prevents setting manufacturing dates in the future, and 
--              ensures a minimum 6-month shelf life.
-- 

CREATE OR REPLACE FUNCTION enforce_batch_dates()
RETURNS TRIGGER 
LANGUAGE plpgsql
AS $$
BEGIN
    -- Rule 1: Prevent "Time Travel" (Manufacturing in the future)
    IF NEW.Mfg_Date > CURRENT_DATE THEN
        RAISE EXCEPTION 'TIME TRAVEL DETECTED: You cannot set a Manufacturing Date (%) in the future!', NEW.Mfg_Date;
    END IF;

    -- Rule 2: Ensure minimum 6-month shelf life
    -- (We add 6 months to the Mfg_Date and compare it to Exp_Date)
    IF NEW.Exp_Date < (NEW.Mfg_Date + INTERVAL '6 months') THEN
        RAISE EXCEPTION 'QUALITY BLOCK: The Expiry Date (%) must be at least 6 months after the Manufacturing Date (%).', NEW.Exp_Date, NEW.Mfg_Date;
    END IF;

    -- If both rules pass, allow the INSERT or UPDATE to finish
    RETURN NEW;
END;
$$;

-- Bind the trigger to fire BEFORE BOTH Inserts and Updates!
CREATE TRIGGER trg_strict_batch_dates
BEFORE INSERT OR UPDATE ON Batch
FOR EACH ROW
EXECUTE FUNCTION enforce_batch_dates();
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{4. The Silent Auditor (\texttt{trg\_audit\_qc\_changes})}
\textbf{Execution Phase:} \texttt{AFTER UPDATE OR DELETE} on \texttt{Product\_Quality\_Check}

\textbf{Why is this important?}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{FDA 21 CFR Part 11 Compliance:} Federal regulations mandate that critical electronic pharmaceutical records cannot be altered or destroyed without generating a permanent, trackable digital paper trail. 
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Immutable Surveillance:} If an employee attempts to secretly change a failing lab grade to a passing one, or deletes a record entirely to hide a mistake, this trigger acts as a hidden security camera. It silently captures the original data, the tampered data, the exact action taken, and the database user's credentials, depositing them into a hidden vault (\texttt{QC\_Audit\_Log}) for forensic review.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
-- 
-- FUNCTION: The Silent Auditor
-- DESCRIPTION: Tracks any modifications or deletions of Lab Results.
--
CREATE OR REPLACE FUNCTION track_qc_changes()
RETURNS TRIGGER 
LANGUAGE plpgsql
AS $$
BEGIN
    -- SCENARIO A: Someone is trying to UPDATE a lab result
    IF TG_OP = 'UPDATE' THEN
        -- If the result actually changed, log the evidence!
        IF OLD.Results IS DISTINCT FROM NEW.Results THEN
            INSERT INTO QC_Audit_Log (Report_ID, Action_Type, Old_Result, New_Result, Changed_By)
            VALUES (OLD.Report_ID, 'UPDATE', OLD.Results, NEW.Results, CURRENT_USER);
        END IF;
        RETURN NEW;

    -- SCENARIO B: Someone is trying to totally DELETE a lab record
    ELSIF TG_OP = 'DELETE' THEN
        INSERT INTO QC_Audit_Log (Report_ID, Action_Type, Old_Result, New_Result, Changed_By)
        VALUES (OLD.Report_ID, 'DELETE', OLD.Results, 'RECORD DESTROYED', CURRENT_USER);
        RETURN OLD;
    END IF;
END;
$$;

-- Bind the trigger to listen AFTER someone touches the QC table
CREATE TRIGGER trg_audit_qc_changes
AFTER UPDATE OR DELETE ON Product_Quality_Check
FOR EACH ROW
EXECUTE FUNCTION track_qc_changes();
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Data Abstraction: Enterprise Views}
In a highly normalized database, business intelligence (BI) tools and frontend dashboards often require data that spans multiple complex joins. To optimize performance and restrict direct access to core transactional tables, PharmaChain-DB utilizes native SQL Views. These act as secure, pre-compiled virtual tables that serve real-time analytics to specific factory departments.

% --- VIEW 1 ---
\subsection{1. Procurement Dashboard: Low Stock Alerts (\texttt{v\_inventory\_shortage})}

\textbf{Why is this important?}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Automated Supply Chain Triggers:} Supply chain managers do not have time to manually calculate the remaining stock of hundreds of chemicals. This view acts as a daily "To-Do List" for the purchasing team, instantly filtering out healthy stock and only showing materials in the danger zone.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Preventing Factory Halts:} By calculating the exact difference between current stock and the defined \texttt{Reorder\_Level}, it tells the procurement team exactly how many units they need to buy to prevent a total manufacturing shutdown.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Data Security:} It allows the purchasing department's software to see inventory deficits without ever granting them direct \texttt{UPDATE} or \texttt{DELETE} permissions on the sensitive \texttt{Warehouse} table.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=SQL]
-- 
-- VIEW: Procurement Dashboard (Low Stock Alert)
-- DESCRIPTION: Instantly shows the purchasing team exactly which 
--              materials have dropped below their safe reorder limit.
-- 

CREATE OR REPLACE VIEW v_inventory_shortage AS
SELECT 
    w.Item_ID,
    m.Material_Name,
    m.Material_Type,
    w.Stock AS Current_Stock,
    m.Reorder_Level AS Minimum_Required,
    (m.Reorder_Level - w.Stock) AS Units_To_Order
FROM Warehouse w
JOIN Material_Master m ON w.Material_ID = m.Material_ID
WHERE w.Stock <= m.Reorder_Level;
\end{lstlisting}

This view instantly aggregates data across six different tables to show exactly when a pill was made, what raw chemicals were mixed into it, its final lab grade, and how much was sold.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Surgical Product Recalls:} If a specific raw material supplier issues a contamination warning, this view allows executives to instantly identify every single finished medicine batch that contains that specific chemical, preventing a massive, uncoordinated market recall.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Cross-Departmental Visibility:} It bridges the gap between Manufacturing, Quality Control, and Sales. Instead of writing complex, 7-table JOIN queries every time management needs a report, they can query this view like a simple spreadsheet.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=SQL]
-- 

-- VIEW: FDA End-to-End Batch Traceability
-- DESCRIPTION: A complete lifecycle map of every batch, showing its 
--              ingredients, QC status, and market sales.
-- 
CREATE OR REPLACE VIEW v_fda_batch_traceability AS
SELECT 
    B.Batch_No,
    PM.Product_Name,
    B.Mfg_Date,
    B.Exp_Date,
    COALESCE(QC.Results, 'UNTESTED') AS QC_Status,
    STRING_AGG(DISTINCT MM.Material_Name, ', ') AS Raw_Materials_Used,
    COALESCE(SUM(FGT.Sale_Qty), 0) AS Total_Sold_To_Market
FROM Batch B
JOIN Product_Master PM ON B.Product_ID = PM.Product_ID
LEFT JOIN Product_Quality_Check QC ON B.Batch_No = QC.Batch_No
LEFT JOIN Material_Dispensing MD ON B.Batch_No = MD.Batch_No
LEFT JOIN Warehouse W ON MD.Item_ID = W.Item_ID
LEFT JOIN Material_Master MM ON W.Material_ID = MM.Material_ID
LEFT JOIN FG_Transaction FGT ON B.Batch_No = FGT.Batch_No
GROUP BY 
    B.Batch_No, 
    PM.Product_Name, 
    B.Mfg_Date, 
    B.Exp_Date, 
    QC.Results;
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{3. Expiry Risk \& Inventory Liability Dashboard (\texttt{v\_inventory\_expiry\_risk})}

\textbf{Why is this important?}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Financial Liability Mitigation:} Pharmaceutical products have strict shelf lives. This view acts as an early warning system, allowing the sales team to heavily discount or push batches that are approaching expiration before they become a total financial loss.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Automated Risk Categorization:} Instead of relying on employees to manually calculate dates, the database natively categorizes inventory into actionable status codes (\texttt{SAFE}, \texttt{WARNING}, \texttt{CRITICAL}, \texttt{EXPIRED}) based on real-time chronological math.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Accurate "Floor Stock" Tracking:} By programmatically subtracting all completed sales from the initial manufactured batch size, executives know exactly how many units are physically sitting unsold in the warehouse at any given second.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=SQL]
-- 
-- VIEW: Expiry Risk & Inventory Liability Dashboard
-- DESCRIPTION: Tracks unsold finished goods, calculates days until 
--              expiry, and flags high-risk inventory using status codes.
-- 

CREATE OR REPLACE VIEW v_inventory_expiry_risk AS
SELECT 
    B.Batch_No,
    PM.Product_Name,
    B.Exp_Date,
    (B.Exp_Date - CURRENT_DATE) AS Days_Remaining,
    
    -- Professional Status Codes
    CASE 
        WHEN (B.Exp_Date - CURRENT_DATE) < 0 THEN 'EXPIRED - DO NOT SELL'
        WHEN (B.Exp_Date - CURRENT_DATE) <= 30 THEN 'CRITICAL - UNDER 30 DAYS'
        WHEN (B.Exp_Date - CURRENT_DATE) <= 90 THEN 'WARNING - UNDER 90 DAYS'
        ELSE 'SAFE'
    END AS Risk_Status,
    
    B.Stock_Qty AS Manufactured_Qty,
    COALESCE(SUM(FGT.Sale_Qty), 0) AS Total_Sold_Qty,
    
    -- Calculate exactly how many units are sitting unsold on the shelf
    (B.Stock_Qty - COALESCE(SUM(FGT.Sale_Qty), 0)) AS Unsold_Inventory

FROM Batch B
JOIN Product_Master PM ON B.Product_ID = PM.Product_ID
LEFT JOIN FG_Transaction FGT ON B.Batch_No = FGT.Batch_No

GROUP BY 
    B.Batch_No, 
    PM.Product_Name, 
    B.Exp_Date, 
    B.Stock_Qty

-- ONLY show batches where we still have unsold inventory
HAVING (B.Stock_Qty - COALESCE(SUM(FGT.Sale_Qty), 0)) > 0

-- Sort the most dangerous (expiring soonest) to the top
ORDER BY Days_Remaining ASC;
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Complex Transactions: Stored Procedures}

\subsection*{Architectural Concept: Stored Procedures vs. Triggers}
While both Stored Procedures and Triggers execute business logic on the database server, their architectural roles are fundamentally different:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Triggers are Reactive (Event-Driven):} A trigger acts as a hidden background guard. It fires automatically in response to a DML event (\texttt{INSERT}, \texttt{UPDATE}, \texttt{DELETE}). The user or application does not explicitly call a trigger.
    \item \textbf{Stored Procedures are Proactive (On-Demand):} A stored procedure is a pre-compiled script called manually by an application or user via the \texttt{CALL} command. They are designed to group multiple, complex database operations into a single \textbf{atomic transaction}. If any part of the procedure fails, the entire transaction rolls back, preventing partial data updates.
\end{itemize}

% --- STORED PROCEDURE 1 ---
\subsection{1. The Emergency Quarantine Protocol (\texttt{execute\_product\_recall})}

\textbf{Why is this important?}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Rapid Crisis Response:} During a catastrophic medicine failure or FDA mandate, executives do not have time to manually update multiple tables. This procedure allows a manager to execute a total factory quarantine with a single line of code.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Atomic Multi-Table Transactions:} This script executes a highly orchestrated sequence: it checks if the batch exists, verifies physical stock, logs the legal recall event, zeroes out the physical warehouse inventory, and overwrites the Quality Control lab records. Grouping this into a procedure ensures that all steps happen together, or none happen at all.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Frontend Abstraction:} The application developers do not need to know the complex relational schema of the database. They simply pass the \texttt{Recall\_ID}, \texttt{Batch\_No}, and \texttt{Reason} to the procedure, and the database handles the heavy lifting safely.
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=SQL]
-- 
-- PROCEDURE: Execute Emergency Product Recall (Tailored)
-- DESCRIPTION: Integrates exactly with the user's Product_Recall table,
--              enforcing all custom constraints and VARCHAR keys.
-- 

CREATE OR REPLACE PROCEDURE execute_product_recall(
    p_recall_id VARCHAR(20),  
    p_batch_no NUMERIC,
    p_reason VARCHAR(255)     
)
LANGUAGE plpgsql
AS $$
DECLARE
    v_unsold_qty NUMERIC;
BEGIN
    -- 1. Find out exactly how many units are currently in the warehouse
    SELECT Stock_Qty INTO v_unsold_qty 
    FROM Batch 
    WHERE Batch_No = p_batch_no;

    -- Safety Check A: Does this batch exist?
    IF v_unsold_qty IS NULL THEN
        RAISE EXCEPTION 'RECALL FAILED: Batch % does not exist in the system.', p_batch_no;
    END IF;

    -- Safety Check B: Satisfy your CHECK (Qty_Recalled > 0) constraint
    IF v_unsold_qty <= 0 THEN
         RAISE EXCEPTION 'RECALL FAILED: Batch % has 0 stock in the warehouse. Nothing left to quarantine!', p_batch_no;
    END IF;

    -- 2. Log the event into YOUR exact table
    INSERT INTO Product_Recall (Recall_ID, Batch_No, Date_Initiated, Reason, Qty_Recalled)
    VALUES (p_recall_id, p_batch_no, CURRENT_DATE, p_reason, v_unsold_qty);

    -- 3. Zero out the warehouse stock
    UPDATE Batch 
    SET Stock_Qty = 0 
    WHERE Batch_No = p_batch_no;

    -- 4. Overwrite the Quality Control lab result to quarantine the batch
    UPDATE Product_Quality_Check 
    SET Results = 'RECALLED' 
    WHERE Batch_No = p_batch_no;

END;
$$;
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Future Scope and Next Steps}
While the current PharmaChain-DB architecture successfully achieves strict normalization and data integrity, a database must evolve as the company grows. The following practical database improvements outline our next steps for making the system faster, safer, and easier to connect to other software.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsection*{1. Automated Email and SMS Alerts}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Active Notifications:} Right now, employees have to manually check the database views to see if raw materials are running low. 
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Database Triggers to Email:} In the future, we can configure the database (using tools like \texttt{pg\_notify}) to automatically send an email or text message to the purchasing manager the exact second a chemical drops below its safe reorder level.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{2. Data Archiving for Faster Performance}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Managing Database Growth:} After five or ten years, tables like \texttt{Production\_Log} and \texttt{Transactions} will contain millions of rows, which can slow down daily queries.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Historical Tables:} We plan to build automated archiving scripts that move very old, completed medicine batches into separate "History" tables. This keeps the main active tables small and lightning-fast, while still legally saving the old data.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{3. Connecting to Web and Mobile Apps (API Integration)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Beyond SQL Queries:} Currently, users interact with the data by running SQL scripts directly.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Building a REST API:} The next step is to wrap the database in a secure API. This will allow frontend web developers to easily build a simple website or mobile app where factory workers can just tap buttons on a screen to insert data, rather than knowing any code.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{4. Materialized Views for Heavy Reports}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Speeding Up Dashboards:} Our \texttt{v\_fda\_batch\_traceability} view joins seven different tables together. If executives run this report multiple times a day on a massive database, it could heavily tax the server's CPU.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Pre-saving the Math:} We can upgrade standard views into "Materialized Views." This means the database will pre-calculate the heavy math overnight and save the results, so when the boss asks for the report in the morning, it loads instantly.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{5. Automated Backups and Disaster Recovery}
\begin{itemize}
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Protecting the Data:} If the factory's main server loses power or a hard drive crashes, years of pharmaceutical records could be lost.
    \item[$\rightarrow$] \textbf{Nightly Snapshots:} We will implement automated database cron jobs (scheduled tasks) that safely copy the entire database every night and send the backup file to a secure, off-site location so it can always be restored.
\end{itemize}
